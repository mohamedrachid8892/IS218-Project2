Example Calculator

This page provides a look at a basic calculator written in Python. In it we will take a look at the four principles of OOP: abstraction, encapsulation, inheritance, and polymorphism.

Required Knowledge

Before continuing, be sure you understand the four principles of OOP. If you don't, take a look at the glossary, where I explain each concept and provide an example.

The Calculator

The repository for the calculator can be found here.

Abstraction

In order to see an example of abstraction in the calculator program, take a look at the calculator class. Each of the calculations is done through the Calculator class. However, you do not see how the calculations are done in their respective classes.

Encapsulation

Encapsulation is the bundling of data with the methods that operate on that data. Encapsulation also restricts direct access to an object's components. For an example of this, take a look at the Calculations History class. Here, the methods of the class operate on the history list. Furthermore, it is impossible to directly access the history member outside of the class.

Inheritance

Inheritance is the concept of basing a class upon another class. This is exemplified in the relationship between the Calculations class and the specific operations classes (multiplication, addition, subtraction, division). In this relationship, the Calculation class is the parent class, and the other arithmetic calculation classes are the child classes.

Polymorphism

Polymorphism describes the concept that different classes can be used with the same interface. Again, this is seen in the Calculations classes and the other four arithmetic classes. The calculation class takes on multiple forms with the different classes.
